Table 2 gives the new OD pairs considered for calculation of accessibility.  These have been replaced from old OD pairs because either there is no exit ramp at that location or they are far away from the ramps under consideration.  These new pairs will give us accurate estimate of travel time.  The distances between these new pairs was calculated from the handbook and compared with the distances from the data provided. This will give the average space mean speed at each station. Then average speed for each freeway segment is obtained by taking averages of the average space mean speed. This means that first the average speed of each detector station is obtained for the peak hour period. Then depending upon OD pairs, the average freeway speed is obtained by summing up the upstream and down stream detectors station.  This will give us the average space mean speed for that freeway section.  As an example, the average speed between Cretin Avenue and Dale Street is shown in Table 1. This average speed is then used to estimate the approximate travel time between the exit ramps. 
If people perceive time differently, then it is necessary to measure accessibility of the system. Greater the accessibility of land is, greater will be its value.  For example, the land value in downtown area is more, as it is more accessible to business areas as compared to suburban areas.  In case of transportation network, people living near freeways have more accessibility.  Accessibility of transportation network is more if time consumed to reach a particular destination is less.  For example if person X has two choices, he can either take route A (congested freeway) or route B (un-congested surface arterial).  The freeway time consists of wait time on ramp and freeway travel time.  Whereas the time on surface arterial consists of wait time at signals and surface street travel time.  Since we are assuming that time to travel on Surface Street is less as compared to freeway, therefore route B is more accessible than route A.
