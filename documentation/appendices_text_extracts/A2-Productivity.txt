APPENDIX 2: Calculation of Consumer's Surplus and Productivity	
For each of the freeway segments the 5-minute flows and the travel times required for each vehicle was considered and the corresponding VKTs and VSTs were estimated. The VKTs and the VSTs for all the 5-minute intervals were added up to get the totals for each of the freeway segments and the ratio between the total VKT and VST gave the productivity for that freeway segment. The step was done for both the metered and the non- metered cases. The changes in flow and travel times on the freeway segments between the metered and the non-metered cases for every corresponding 5 minute let us calculate the change in consumer surplus for each 5-minute interval for any freeway segment (as explained above). Addition of all the changes (over all the 5 minute periods) gave the total change in consumer surplus for that freeway segment for the entire metered period. 
Table 2 is a sample of calculations for one ramp (Olson Highway). Referring to Table 2, the first time interval starts at 14:55 and the last one is at 18:10.  As can be seen, the first column is the ramp flows, the second one is ramp delay i.e., the number of seconds each car waits on the ramp. The third column is the total delay of the vehicle including ramp wait. The fifth column is the VKT (the product of the ramp flows with the length of the ramp in km, here it is 0.468 km). The last column is the VST (the product of the flows and the total delay on the ramp). 

There is some need to explain the calculation in the third column. The values in this column include the stopped delay that each car has to experience on the ramp while being in queue plus the time that it takes in order to reach it's position in the queue from the start of the ramp. This was calculated as follows:

The rates at which the cars were released from the ramps onto the highway depend on the red times. For each of the ramps, the red times were averaged across the whole metering period. For the example shown in Table 2, (For the Olson Highway), the red times for the entire period of metering was averaged and found to be 3.19 seconds. So each car is released every 3.19 seconds. If, for the first 5-minute interval, the ramp wait was 8.2 seconds, then there must have been (8.2/3.19 =) 2.57 cars ahead of each car arriving in that 5-min interval (assuming that one car is allowed to leave every 3.19 seconds). Inter-car distance has been assumed as 6.1m and so the distance that each car has to traverse while being in queue is equal to 2.57*6.1 = 15.68 m and the delay while covering this distance is already covered in the ramp wait. So the car has to cover the remaining distance (excluding the queue length) at a speed of 40 km/h (assumed). For bringing in some temporal uniformity, we have divided the total ramp wait (in queue) by the total number of 5-minute time intervals i.e., 40 and the result by 3.19 (for one car is released every 3.19 seconds) to get the average number of cars in the queue. In the sample shown in table 2, the average number of cars at any point of time in queue is 7.1 (each of which is released every 3.19 seconds) and so the rest of the distance is equal to the length of the ramp minus the length of  7.1 cars and the time required to cover that distance is equal to {468-(7.1*6.1)}/11.11 = 38.2 secs. [the cars travel the distance at 40 km/h=11.11m/s]. So the total time that a single car waits (in the first 5 minute interval) is equal to (8.2+38.2=) 46.43 seconds. This way all the total delays on the ramp are calculated. The delays multiplied by the flows give the VSTs and the flows multiplied by the length of the ramp give the VKTs. We sum up all the VKTs and VSTs to get the value for the entire period of metering and then the ratio VKT/VST gives the productivity. In the example shown above, the productivity of the ramp is equal to 0.0076. Likewise we find the productivities of all the ramps. However, for some ramps, the flows might spill over the ramps (when the total length of the vehicles in queue exceeds the ramp length) and then the total delay would simply be the ramp waits while in the queue. This has been incorporated in another column titled, "Average travel time including ramp waits (corrected)". 
For the non metered case, the total delay is simply the time required to travel the ramp length at 40 km/hour. And the rest of the calculations are identical to the method described above. For the ramp shown already (Olson Hwy) the calculations for the non metered case are shown in Table 3 of the appendix.
